## Activities by Topic Category

### Logic & Reasoning

---

#### Odd One Out Cards
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.21
- **Used in**: Week 1 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Classic Zvonkin activity. His kids were 3-4; for 7-8 year olds, use more attributes per object and accept increasingly sophisticated reasoning.
- **Materials**: Sets of 4 cards/objects where any one could be the "odd one out" depending on reasoning. Use picture cards, attribute blocks, or hand-drawn sets.
- **How it works**: Show 4 items. Ask "Which one doesn't belong?" Accept ANY answer with a valid reason. Then ask: "Can you find a reason for a different one to be the odd one out?" The goal is for kids to realize there's no single right answer -- the reasoning is what matters.
- **Key facilitation**: Do NOT validate one answer over another. If a kid says "the red one, because the others are blue" and another says "the circle, because the others have corners," both are equally correct. Push for a third and fourth reason.
- **Extensions**: Have kids create their own Odd One Out sets for classmates to solve. This is harder than solving and reveals deep thinking about attributes.

---

#### Venn Diagram Sorting
- **Zvonkin ref**: Intersecting sets, referenced throughout; see also p.21 on classification.
- **Used in**: Week 1 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Use two overlapping hula hoops or yarn circles on the floor. Physical, large-scale. Zvonkin found even very young kids grasp set intersection when they can walk into the circles.
- **Materials**: Two hula hoops or yarn loops, a collection of 20-30 objects with varied attributes (color, size, shape, texture). Label cards for categories.
- **How it works**: Place two hoops overlapping on the floor. Label one "Red things" and one "Big things." Hand each kid 2-3 objects. They place objects in the correct region. The overlapping region is the key discovery: "Where does something go if it's red AND big?"
- **Key facilitation**: Start with obvious categories. Once kids get it, let THEM choose the categories. "What should our two circles be?" Watch for the moment a kid discovers the overlap region on their own.
- **Extensions**: Add a third circle (gets complex fast). Or use "NOT" categories: "Not round" and "Not blue."

---

#### Who Is Booter? (Nonsense Logic)
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.168
- **Used in**: Week 3 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin used made-up words to strip away real-world knowledge and force pure logical reasoning. 7-8 year olds love the silliness and can handle 2-3 step deductions.
- **Materials**: Printed puzzle cards with nonsense-word logic problems. Whiteboard for working through clues together.
- **How it works**: "All booters are green. Snik is a booter. Is Snik green?" Start simple, build complexity. "All mips have tails. All zogs are mips. Does a zog have a tail?" The nonsense words prevent kids from using real-world knowledge -- they must follow the logic.
- **Key facilitation**: Let kids argue. If one kid says "We don't know what a mip is!" that's a great moment. The whole point is: you don't need to know what it IS, you just follow the rules. Don't explain this -- let them wrestle with it.
- **Extensions**: Kids write their own nonsense logic puzzles for each other.

---

#### True/False Statements
- **Zvonkin ref**: Logical reasoning activities throughout; see p.166 on deduction.
- **Used in**: Week 3 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Start with obvious ("The sky is green" -- false) and quickly move to tricky ("All dogs have four legs" -- what about a three-legged dog?). 7-8 year olds can handle ambiguity if you let them debate.
- **Materials**: Statement cards, "True" and "False" signs for two corners of the room.
- **How it works**: Read a statement. Kids move to the True corner or False corner. Then: the interesting part. Ask a kid from each side to explain. Let them persuade each other. Some statements are deliberately ambiguous.
- **Key facilitation**: Include statements where reasonable people disagree. "A square is a rectangle" -- this will split the room and generate real mathematical argument. Do not resolve the argument. Let it hang.
- **Extensions**: Introduce "Always true / Sometimes true / Never true" as three categories.

---

#### Logical Deduction Puzzles
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.166
- **Used in**: Week 3 (can substitute for either Main Activity)
- **Age notes**: Grid-style logic puzzles are too abstract for most 7-year-olds. Instead, use story-based deduction: "Three kids each have a pet. Use these clues to figure out who has which pet."
- **Materials**: Printed puzzle sheets, colored chips or tokens to represent people/objects.
- **How it works**: Read the scenario aloud. Give kids physical tokens to move around as they reason. "We know Sam doesn't have the cat. Move Sam's token away from the cat." Work the first one together, then release pairs to try the next.
- **Key facilitation**: The physical tokens are essential. Without them, kids lose track of what they've figured out. Let kids use whatever tracking method they invent.

---

#### Liar & Truth-Teller Game
- **Zvonkin ref**: Classic logic puzzle format, extends the nonsense logic of p.168.
- **Used in**: Week 3 (Game)
- **Age notes**: Simplified version. One kid is secretly assigned "always tells truth" or "always lies." Others ask yes/no questions to figure out which. 7-8 year olds find this hilarious and surprisingly challenging.
- **Materials**: Two cards: "Truth-Teller" and "Liar."
- **How it works**: One volunteer draws a card (secret). The group asks yes/no questions. The volunteer must answer honestly or dishonestly per their card. Group tries to determine which card they have. Play multiple rounds with different volunteers.
- **Key facilitation**: Start with a demo where you are the truth-teller/liar. Model the reasoning: "She said the sky is purple. Is that true? No. So she must be the liar." Let kids develop the strategy of asking questions they already know the answer to.

### Geometry & Spatial

---

#### Pegboard Symmetry
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.23
- **Used in**: Week 2 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin used geoboards with young kids. 7-8 year olds can handle the line-of-symmetry concept and produce complex symmetric designs.
- **Materials**: Geoboards (or printed dot grids) and rubber bands. One per kid or one per pair. Pre-mark a line of symmetry with a rubber band or drawn line.
- **How it works**: One side of the line has a rubber band shape. Kids must create the mirror image on the other side. Start with simple shapes (L, T, square) and increase complexity. After guided practice, let kids create their own challenges for a partner.
- **Key facilitation**: Mistakes are visible and self-correcting -- a kid can SEE that their shape doesn't look symmetric. Ask "Does it look the same on both sides?" rather than pointing out errors.
- **Extensions**: Diagonal line of symmetry (much harder). Or: two lines of symmetry (4-fold).

---

#### Mirror Reflections
- **Zvonkin ref**: Relates to symmetry work; see also p.178 on perspective.
- **Used in**: Week 2 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Highly tactile and immediately engaging. Use real mirrors, not drawings of reflections.
- **Materials**: Small rectangular mirrors (one per kid), printed images/letters/shapes to reflect, blank paper for drawing predictions.
- **How it works**: Place a mirror on the edge of a half-image. "What does the whole thing look like?" Then: place a mirror in the middle of a word. "What do you see?" Explore which capital letters look the same in a mirror (A, H, M, T, U, V, W, X, Y) and which don't (B, C, D, etc.).
- **Key facilitation**: Let kids just PLAY with the mirrors first. 2-3 minutes of free exploration. They'll discover things you didn't plan. Then channel it.
- **Extensions**: Two mirrors at an angle -- kaleidoscope effect. How does the number of reflections change with the angle?

---

#### Mobius Bands
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.49
- **Used in**: Week 7 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin calls this one of the most reliably astonishing activities. Works at any age. 7-8 year olds can make their own and will gasp when they cut one in half.
- **Materials**: Strips of paper (about 2 cm wide, 30 cm long), tape, scissors, markers.
- **How it works**: Step 1: Make a regular loop (tape ends together). Draw a line down the middle all the way around. How many sides does it have? (Two.) Cut along the line. (Two separate loops.) Step 2: Make a Mobius strip (twist one end 180 degrees before taping). Draw a line down the middle. How many sides? (One -- the line meets itself.) Now cut along the line. (One big loop, not two!)
- **Key facilitation**: Have kids PREDICT before each step. "What do you think will happen when we cut?" Write predictions on the board. The surprise is the lesson. Don't explain WHY it works -- just let them sit with the wonder.
- **Extensions**: Cut a Mobius strip at one-third width instead of one-half. Two interlocked loops. Mind-blowing.

#### Topology: Faces, Vertices, Edges
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.296
- **Used in**: Week 7 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Euler's formula (V - E + F = 2) is too abstract to teach directly, but kids can discover the pattern by counting. Use physical 3D shapes, not pictures.
- **Materials**: Wooden or plastic geometric solids (cube, tetrahedron, triangular prism, rectangular prism, etc.), recording sheets with columns for Faces/Vertices/Edges.
- **How it works**: Hands each group 3-4 solids. "Count the faces (flat parts), vertices (pointy corners), and edges (where two faces meet). Write them down." Once all groups have data, compile on the board. "Do you notice anything?" Guide toward V - E + F = 2, but if they don't find it, that's fine. The counting itself is the activity.
- **Key facilitation**: Kids will miscount. That's OK. When groups get different numbers for the same shape, ask them to recount together. This is real mathematical practice -- checking your work because the pattern breaks if the count is wrong.

---

#### Euler's Formula Hunt
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.296
- **Used in**: Week 7 (Game)
- **Age notes**: This is the game version of the Faces/Vertices/Edges activity. After collecting data in Activity 2, teams race to test the pattern on new shapes.
- **Materials**: Additional 3D solids or printed nets that kids fold, recording sheets.
- **How it works**: Each team gets a new shape. Count V, E, F. Does the pattern hold? First team to verify gets a point. Introduce a "trick" shape (a cylinder -- no vertices, what happens to the formula?).

---

#### Floor Plans
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.172
- **Used in**: Week 9 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin found that translating 3D space to 2D representation is a key cognitive leap. 7-8 year olds can draw simple floor plans of rooms they know.
- **Materials**: Graph paper, pencils, rulers. Toy furniture or blocks for building a model room first.
- **How it works**: Start by building a simple room with blocks on a table (4 walls, a door, a bed, a table). Then: "Draw what this room looks like from above, like a bird looking down." Provide graph paper. Then reverse: give a floor plan and have kids build the room from blocks.
- **Key facilitation**: The first attempts will be strange -- 3D objects drawn in 3D on the plan, walls shown from the side, etc. Don't correct. Ask: "If a bird flew over this room, would it see the bed sticking up like that?"

#### What Does the Other Person See?
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.178
- **Used in**: Week 9 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Perspective-taking. Piaget showed this is developing at ages 7-8 -- some kids will get it immediately, others will struggle. Both responses are normal and useful.
- **Materials**: A simple scene built from blocks or toy figures on a table. Paper and crayons.
- **How it works**: Two kids sit on opposite sides of a table with a block scene. Each draws what THEY see. Then they swap seats and compare. "Why does it look different?" Then the hard version: "Without moving, draw what YOUR PARTNER sees from their side."
- **Key facilitation**: This is cognitively demanding. Some kids will just draw what they see again. Don't push too hard -- the seed is planted. The act of comparing drawings is where the learning happens.

---

#### Paper Cutting / Snowflakes
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.123
- **Used in**: Week 2 (alternate activity) or Week 10 (celebration station)
- **Age notes**: Fold-and-cut activities are accessible and beautiful. Connects to symmetry.
- **Materials**: Paper squares, scissors. Colored paper is more fun.
- **How it works**: Fold a paper in half. Cut a shape from the folded edge. "Predict what it will look like when you open it." Open and check. Then fold in quarters and repeat. Then: "Can you cut a shape that will make a star? A heart? A circle?"
- **Key facilitation**: The prediction is everything. Don't let kids just cut randomly (though some initial free exploration is fine). Always: fold, predict, cut, check.

---

#### Tangrams
- **Zvonkin ref**: Spatial reasoning, general.
- **Used in**: Week 9 (Game)
- **Age notes**: Classic spatial puzzle. 7-8 year olds can solve outlines with the pieces next to the outline. Harder: silhouette only.
- **Materials**: Tangram sets (one per kid or pair), printed outline cards at varying difficulty.
- **How it works**: Start with outlines where the internal piece divisions are visible. Progress to silhouettes-only. Time challenges for kids who are ready.
- **Key facilitation**: Tangrams are self-differentiating. Struggling kids work on easy outlines; advanced kids tackle silhouettes. No modifications needed.

---

#### Building from Diagrams
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.270
- **Used in**: Week 9 (alternate activity)
- **Age notes**: 2D-to-3D translation. Give kids a picture of a block structure and ask them to build it.
- **Materials**: Interlocking cubes or wooden blocks, printed diagrams of 3D structures from different angles.
- **How it works**: Show a diagram. "Build this." Start with structures made of 3-5 cubes, increase complexity. Then reverse: one kid builds a hidden structure and draws it; partner builds from the drawing.

---

#### Symmetric Drawing Race
- **Zvonkin ref**: Symmetry activities, p.23.
- **Used in**: Week 2 (Game)
- **Age notes**: Fast-paced and fun. Pairs compete to complete symmetric drawings.
- **Materials**: Printed half-images on grid paper, pencils, timer.
- **How it works**: Each pair gets a half-image on a grid. They must draw the symmetric other half. First pair to finish correctly wins the round. Do 5-6 rounds with increasing difficulty.

### Combinatorics & Probability

---

#### Tower of Hanoi
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.55
- **Used in**: Week 4 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin used this with very young children. 7-8 year olds can solve 3-4 discs and begin to reason about strategy. The key discovery: the minimum number of moves doubles (roughly) with each new disc.
- **Materials**: Tower of Hanoi sets (commercial or DIY with cardboard discs and 3 pencils stuck in clay). One set per pair.
- **How it works**: Rules: move one disc at a time, never place a larger disc on a smaller one, move the whole tower from peg A to peg C. Start with 2 discs (3 moves minimum). Then 3 discs (7 moves). Then 4 discs (15 moves). Record minimum moves on the board: 2, 3, 7, 15. "Do you see a pattern?"
- **Key facilitation**: Let kids struggle. Don't teach the algorithm. The feeling of "I figured it out!" is the point. Some kids will solve 3 discs in 20 moves before finding the 7-move solution. That's fine -- they're learning through exploration. If a kid claims to have solved it in fewer than the minimum, have them demonstrate for the group.
- **Extensions**: "How many moves for 5 discs?" (31.) "Can you predict 6?" Let them conjecture.

---

#### Dice Probability Experiments
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.66, p.104
- **Used in**: Week 5 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin emphasizes that probability is counterintuitive at every age. Don't teach formulas. Let data surprise them.
- **Materials**: Pairs of dice (2 per kid or pair), recording sheets with a number line from 2-12, crayons or markers.
- **How it works**: "Roll two dice and add them. Color in the bar above that number on your number line. Do this 30 times." After everyone has 30 rolls, compare charts. "What numbers came up the most? The least? Why?" 7 should dominate. "Why does 7 come up more than 2?" Don't explain with a table of combinations -- let kids theorize.
- **Key facilitation**: Some kids will say "7 is lucky." Don't correct immediately. Ask: "How many ways can you make 7 with two dice? How many ways can you make 2?" If they can't figure it out, leave it. Plant the seed. Come back to it during Cool-Down.
- **Extensions**: "Is this game fair? I win if the sum is even, you win if it's odd." (Fair.) "I win if the sum is over 7, you win if it's under 7." (What about exactly 7?) "I win if both dice show the same number, you win if they're different." (Very unfair.)

---

#### Fair vs. Unfair Games
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.104
- **Used in**: Week 5 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Builds directly on the dice experiments. Kids test games to determine if they're fair.
- **Materials**: Various games/spinners rigged to be fair or unfair. Dice, coins, spinners divided into unequal sections.
- **How it works**: Present 3-4 simple games. "Play each one 10 times. Is it fair? How do you know?" Include one that looks fair but isn't and one that looks unfair but is.
- **Key facilitation**: "Fair" means "both players have the same chance of winning." This is a hard concept. Some kids will say a game is unfair because they lost. Ask: "If you played 100 times, do you think you'd each win about 50?"

---

#### Pig (Dice Game)
- **Zvonkin ref**: Probability and strategy game, complements p.66.
- **Used in**: Week 5 (Game)
- **Age notes**: Simple rules, real strategy, genuine excitement. Perfect for 7-8 year olds.
- **Materials**: One die per pair, paper to keep score.
- **How it works**: On your turn, roll a die repeatedly, adding each roll to your turn total. You can stop anytime and bank your points. BUT if you roll a 1, your turn total becomes zero and your turn ends. First to 50 (or 100) wins.
- **Key facilitation**: The strategy question is: "When should you stop?" There's no single right answer, which makes it perfect for math circle. Let kids develop and argue about strategies. "I always stop after 3 rolls." "I stop when I have 15." All valid approaches.

---

#### Nim & Takeaway Games
- **Zvonkin ref**: Strategy games, related to Tower of Hanoi (p.55).
- **Used in**: Week 4 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Simple rules, deep strategy. 7-8 year olds can discover the winning strategy for small versions.
- **Materials**: Counters, coins, or stones (15-20 per pair).
- **How it works**: Place 15 counters in a pile. Two players alternate taking 1, 2, or 3 counters. The player who takes the last counter wins (or loses -- play both versions). "Can you find a strategy that always wins?"
- **Key facilitation**: Start with a tiny game (5 counters). Let kids play 10+ rounds. Some will discover that leaving your opponent with 4 is a winning position. Then: "Does that idea help with 15 counters?" Don't teach the strategy. Let them find it.
- **Extensions**: Change the rules: take 1 or 2 (not 3). Multiple piles (classic Nim). These variations let you revisit the same mathematical idea in fresh packaging.

---

#### Tournament Play
- **Zvonkin ref**: General game framework.
- **Used in**: Week 4 (Game)
- **Age notes**: After learning Tower of Hanoi and Nim, hold a mini tournament.
- **Materials**: Nim sets, timer, bracket chart on the board.
- **How it works**: Single-elimination Nim tournament. Quick rounds (small pile sizes = fast games). Kids who are eliminated become referees/judges for remaining matches. Final match is played on the big table with the whole group watching.
- **Key facilitation**: Keep it light. Celebrate clever moves, not just wins. "Did you see what she did? She left exactly 4! Why do you think she did that?"

---

#### Pouring Water Puzzles
- **Zvonkin ref**: Classic puzzle, combinatorial reasoning.
- **Used in**: Week 4 (alternate activity)
- **Age notes**: "You have a 5-liter jug and a 3-liter jug. How do you measure exactly 4 liters?" Use actual water and containers for maximum engagement.
- **Materials**: Measuring cups or labeled containers, water, a tub or tray for spills, recording sheets.
- **How it works**: Start with the easy version: 3-liter and 5-liter jugs, measure exactly 4 liters. Work up to harder versions. Kids record each pour as a step.
- **Key facilitation**: Real water is essential. The mess is worth it. Kids will try things they'd never attempt on paper.

---

#### Counting Rectangles
- **Zvonkin ref**: Combinatorial counting.
- **Used in**: Week 6 (alternate activity)
- **Age notes**: "How many rectangles can you find in this grid?" Deceptively hard -- kids always miss some.
- **Materials**: Printed grids (2x2, 2x3, 3x3), colored pencils for outlining found rectangles.
- **How it works**: Start with a 2x2 grid. "How many rectangles?" (9, if you count all sizes including the full grid.) Most kids will find 4-6 at first. Encourage systematic counting. Move to larger grids.
- **Key facilitation**: The key question is: "How do you know you found them all?" This is the heart of combinatorial thinking -- not just counting, but systematic counting.

### Number Sense & Patterns

---

#### Magic Squares
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.129
- **Used in**: Week 6 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin found magic squares captivating for kids. 3x3 magic squares using digits 1-9 are appropriate for 7-8 year olds. Provide the magic sum (15) and some starter numbers.
- **Materials**: Printed 3x3 grids, number tiles 1-9 (moveable -- don't make kids write and erase), pencils for recording solutions.
- **How it works**: "Place the numbers 1-9 in a 3x3 grid so every row, column, and diagonal adds to 15." Give them tiles to move around. Start with 2-3 numbers pre-placed to reduce difficulty. Remove scaffolding as kids succeed.
- **Key facilitation**: The tiles are crucial -- kids need to experiment without commitment. If they have to erase, they'll stop trying. Moveable tiles say: "It's OK to try and rearrange." If stuck: "What has to go in the center?" (5, because it appears in 4 lines and must be the middle value.)
- **Extensions**: "How many different solutions are there?" (Essentially one, up to rotation/reflection -- 8 total.) 4x4 magic squares for kids who crush the 3x3.

---

#### Gauss's Sum
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.209
- **Used in**: Week 6 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: The story: young Gauss was told to add 1+2+3+...+100 and found the answer instantly. The trick: pair 1+100, 2+99, 3+98... each pair sums to 101, and there are 50 pairs. 50 x 101 = 5050. For 7-8 year olds, start with 1+2+...+10.
- **Materials**: Cuisenaire rods or interlocking cubes, paper for recording.
- **How it works**: Tell the Gauss story. Then: "Can you add 1+2+3+...+10 without adding each number one at a time?" Give kids Cuisenaire rods -- build a staircase from 1 to 10. "Can you rearrange the staircase into a rectangle?" Two staircases fit together into a 10x11 rectangle (110 total), so one staircase is 55.
- **Key facilitation**: The physical staircase-to-rectangle transformation is the key insight. If kids don't see it, give them TWO sets of rods and ask them to make a rectangle. The formula doesn't matter. The visual discovery matters.
- **Extensions**: "What about 1+2+...+20? Can you predict without building?"

---

#### Number Pattern Bingo
- **Zvonkin ref**: Pattern recognition, general.
- **Used in**: Week 6 (Game)
- **Age notes**: Bingo format is familiar and low-stakes. The math is in predicting the next number in a sequence.
- **Materials**: Printed bingo cards with numbers, sequence clues read by the leader.
- **How it works**: Leader reads a sequence: "2, 4, 6, 8, ..." Kids mark the next number (10) if it's on their card. Sequences include: counting by 2s, 3s, 5s; doubling; Fibonacci-style (1,1,2,3,5,...); triangular numbers (1,3,6,10,...). Cards are designed so every sequence has its answer on every card, but in different positions.
- **Key facilitation**: After each round, ask: "How did you figure out the next number?" Different strategies are interesting. Some kids see the pattern visually, others calculate.

---

#### Odd/Even Explorations
- **Zvonkin ref**: Number theory basics, referenced throughout.
- **Used in**: Week 1 (alternate activity) or Week 6 (warm-up)
- **Age notes**: Most 7-8 year olds know even/odd. Push further: "What happens when you add two odd numbers? Two even? One of each?" Let them discover the rules through examples.
- **Materials**: Counters (to physically pair up and check for a leftover), recording sheets.
- **How it works**: "Is 7 even or odd? How do you know?" Pair up 7 counters: 3 pairs with 1 leftover = odd. "Now: 3+5 = 8. Odd + odd = even? Does that ALWAYS work?" Test many cases. Record results. Build a table of rules.
- **Key facilitation**: The question "Does it ALWAYS work?" is the mathematical heart. Kids who test 3 cases and declare a rule are doing something. Kids who ask "But what about really big numbers?" are doing mathematics.

---

#### Number Sequences
- **Zvonkin ref**: Pattern work throughout.
- **Used in**: Week 6 (alternate activity)
- **Age notes**: "What comes next: 1, 4, 9, 16, ...?" Start easy (counting by 2s) and build to square numbers, triangular numbers, and Fibonacci.
- **Materials**: Printed sequence cards with blanks to fill, interlocking cubes for building visual patterns.
- **How it works**: Build visual patterns with cubes: 1, 1+3=4, 4+5=9 (square numbers as growing L-shapes). "Draw the next one. How many cubes will you need?"
- **Key facilitation**: Always make it physical first, then numeric. The pattern in the objects is easier to see than the pattern in the numbers.

---

#### Breaking Codes / Ciphers
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.145
- **Used in**: Week 8 (Main Activity 1)
- **Age notes**: Zvonkin used simple substitution codes. 7-8 year olds can handle Caesar ciphers (shift each letter by a fixed amount) and simple symbol substitution.
- **Materials**: Printed cipher wheels (two concentric circles with letters), coded messages to decrypt, blank paper for encoding.
- **How it works**: Introduce the Caesar cipher: A becomes D, B becomes E, etc. (shift of 3). Give kids a short encoded message to crack. Then let them encode their own messages for a partner.
- **Key facilitation**: The "aha" moment is when a kid figures out the shift by looking at letter frequency or short words ("the" = "wkh" in a shift-3 cipher). Don't teach frequency analysis formally -- just let them notice.
- **Extensions**: Shift of unknown amount (kids must figure out the shift). Symbol substitution (harder). "Can you make a code that's NOT a Caesar cipher?"

---

#### Code-Breaking Challenge
- **Zvonkin ref**: p.145
- **Used in**: Week 8 (Main Activity 2)
- **Age notes**: Teams race to decode messages. Combines the cipher skills from Activity 1 with teamwork and urgency.
- **Materials**: Encoded messages at varying difficulty, answer sheets, timer.
- **How it works**: Each team gets an envelope with 3-4 encoded messages, easiest to hardest. Different cipher types: Caesar shift, symbol substitution, reversed words, number-for-letter (1=A, 2=B...). First team to decode all messages wins.
- **Key facilitation**: Stagger difficulty so every team solves at least the first one. The hardest message should be solvable by zero or one team -- that's what makes it exciting.

---

#### Secret Message Relay
- **Zvonkin ref**: Cipher games, p.145.
- **Used in**: Week 8 (Game)
- **Age notes**: Encoding under time pressure is harder (and more fun) than decoding.
- **Materials**: Cipher wheels, paper, pencils, timer.
- **How it works**: Relay race. Each team has a message to encode. Kid 1 encodes the first 3 letters, passes to Kid 2 for the next 3, etc. Then they pass the encoded message to another team to decode. First team to decode the other team's message wins.

---

#### Geometric Number Patterns
- **Zvonkin ref**: Visual patterns, relates to p.209.
- **Used in**: Week 6 (alternate/extension)
- **Age notes**: Square numbers, triangular numbers, and other figurate numbers shown as physical arrangements of dots or cubes.
- **Materials**: Counters or dot stickers, printed triangular/square dot arrays.
- **How it works**: "Make a triangle with 1 counter. Now grow it: 1, 3, 6, 10... How many counters for the next triangle?" Same with squares: 1, 4, 9, 16. "What's the connection between the square number pattern and the odd number pattern?" (Each new square adds the next odd number: 1, 1+3, 4+5, 9+7...)
- **Key facilitation**: BUILD them. Don't draw them. The physical act of adding a new row to a triangle and counting the new dots is where the understanding lives.

---

#### Permutations
- **Zvonkin ref**: Combinatorial thinking.
- **Used in**: Week 5 (alternate activity)
- **Age notes**: "How many ways can 3 kids line up?" Start with 2 kids (2 ways), then 3 kids (6 ways), then 4 kids (24 ways -- if they can handle it).
- **Materials**: Name cards for 3-4 volunteers, recording sheet for all arrangements.
- **How it works**: Use actual kids standing up and rearranging. Record each arrangement. "Are we sure we found them all?" The systematic approach: "Kid A is first. Who can be second? Who's third? OK, now Kid A is first again, but swap the other two..."
- **Key facilitation**: The physical movement makes this accessible. Kids standing up and shuffling is more engaging than moving cards on a desk. The key question is always: "How do you know you found ALL of them?"

## Week 10: Celebration & Showcase

Week 10 is not a teaching session. It's a celebration of 10 weeks of mathematical thinking.

**Structure**:
- **Stations (30 min)**: Set up 4-5 stations with favorite activities from the previous 9 weeks. Kids rotate freely. Include: Tangrams, Nim, Magic Squares, Mobius bands (there are always new kids who missed it or returners who want to try the 1/3-width cut), and a "make your own Odd One Out" creation station.
- **Gallery Walk (15 min)**: Display work from the whole 10 weeks: Venn diagrams, symmetric designs, journal quotes, magic square solutions, decoded messages. Kids tour with a partner.
- **Free Play & Closing (15 min)**: Kids choose any game or activity. Leader makes brief closing remarks. Ring the bell one final time.

**Materials**: Everything from previous weeks, display boards or wall space, tape.

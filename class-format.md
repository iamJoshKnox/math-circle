# Math Circle Class Format

**Ages**: 7-8 (2nd grade) | **Group size**: 9-15 kids | **Duration**: 60 minutes | **Span**: 8-10 weeks

Inspired by Alexander Zvonkin's *Math from Three to Seven*. This is the reusable session template. Pair it with `activity-bank.md` for weekly content.

---

## Session Structure at a Glance

| Segment | Duration | Description |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Opening Bell** | 5 min | Ritual start (bell/chime), warm-up oral puzzle or "number talk" |
| **Main Activity 1** | 15 min | Hands-on or guided exploration of the week's core topic |
| **Main Activity 2** | 15 min | Second activity -- deeper dive, variation, or partner/team challenge |
| **Game / Play** | 15 min | Structured math game reinforcing the session's theme |
| **Cool-Down & Journal** | 5 min | Kids share one thing they noticed/wondered; leader notes observations |
| **Chromebook Bonus** | 5 min | Introduce the week's browser game for independent play later |

---

## Detailed Segment Guide

### 1. Opening Bell (5 min)

**What happens**: Ring a bell, chime, or singing bowl. This is a ritual -- same sound every week. It signals "math circle brain is on." Then pose a short oral puzzle or number talk.

**Example warm-ups**:
- "I'm thinking of two numbers that add to 10. One is 3 more than the other. What are they?"
- Show a dot pattern briefly, cover it. "How many did you see? How did you see them?"
- "What's the odd one out: 2, 5, 10, 12?" (There's no single right answer -- that's the point.)

**Facilitation notes**:
- Do NOT call on the first hand. Wait. Count silently to 10. Let more kids think.
- Accept multiple answers. Ask "How did you think about it?" not "Is that right?"
- If a kid gives a wrong answer, say "Interesting -- can you tell me more?" or "Does anyone see it differently?" Never say "No." (*Zvonkin principle: No explaining away.*)
- Keep it brisk. This is a warm-up, not a lesson. Cut it off even if not everyone has shared.

> **Zvonkin principle at work**: *Questions > Answers.* The warm-up models that math circle is a place where we think out loud, not a place where we perform correct answers.

---

### 2. Main Activity 1 (15 min)

**What happens**: Introduce the week's core topic through a hands-on exploration. Kids should be touching, building, drawing, or sorting something physical within the first 2 minutes. The leader poses a question or challenge, then steps back.

**Structure options**:
- **Whole group guided exploration**: Leader poses a question, kids work at tables with manipulatives, leader circulates. Good for new concepts.
- **Station rotation**: 2-3 stations, small groups rotate. Good for weeks with multiple related activities.
- **Story/scenario launch**: Frame the math inside a story. "A dragon has locked a princess in a tower made of 3 discs..." (*Zvonkin principle: Vary the packaging.*)

**Facilitation notes**:
- Prepare materials in advance. Bags or trays per table. Transitions kill momentum.
- State the challenge clearly, then stop talking. Write it on the board or a card if needed.
- Circulate and observe. Kneel to kid height. Ask questions: "What have you tried?" "What do you notice?" "Can you show me?"
- Resist the urge to correct. If a kid builds an asymmetric "symmetric" pattern, ask: "If you folded this in half, what would happen?" Don't say: "That's not symmetric." (*Zvonkin principle: Discovery is the goal.*)
- If a kid finishes early: "Can you find another way?" or "Can you make it harder?"
- If a kid is stuck: Pair them with someone, or simplify the challenge ("Try it with just 3 instead of 5").

> **Zvonkin principle at work**: *Manipulatives matter.* Every Main Activity 1 should involve physical objects. Pegboards, mirrors, dice, blocks, cut paper, string -- whatever the week calls for.

---

### 3. Main Activity 2 (15 min)

**What happens**: A second activity that either deepens the first or introduces a variation. This is where partner/team challenges work well, because kids have had 15 minutes to warm up to the topic.

**Structure options**:
- **Partner challenge**: Pairs work on a harder version of Activity 1.
- **Team competition**: Tables compete (gently) on a timed challenge.
- **Gallery walk**: Kids post their work from Activity 1 and tour each other's solutions.
- **Reversal**: "In Activity 1, I gave you the rule and you found examples. Now I'll give you examples -- can you find the rule?"

**Facilitation notes**:
- This is where you can push harder. Kids who are ready will go deeper; kids who are still working on the basics can keep at the Activity 1 level.
- For groups of 9-15, pair work is better than groups of 3-4 at this age. Triads create an odd-one-out socially. Pairs keep everyone engaged.
- If the energy is flagging, pivot to the Game early. Don't force a dead activity.
- If the energy is electric and kids are deep in discovery, let it run long and shorten the Game. (*Zvonkin principle: Let chaos happen.*)

> **Zvonkin principle at work**: *Vary the packaging.* Activity 2 is the same mathematical idea as Activity 1, but it looks and feels different. Classification with colored shapes in Activity 1 becomes classification of animals in Activity 2. Same logic, new story.

---

### 4. Game / Play (15 min)

**What happens**: A structured math game that reinforces the session's theme. Games should be genuinely fun -- not "math practice disguised as a game." Kids should want to play again.

**Game selection criteria**:
- Quick to teach (under 2 minutes of rules).
- Multiple rounds possible in 15 minutes.
- Some element of strategy, not pure luck.
- Playable in pairs or small groups (not whole-class-takes-turns).
- Losable. If everyone always wins, it's not a game.

**Facilitation notes**:
- Demonstrate the game with one volunteer while everyone watches. Play 2-3 rounds publicly before releasing kids to play on their own.
- Have the game set up and ready before the session. Distribute materials fast.
- Circulate and play a round with different groups. Model being a good sport. Model strategic thinking aloud: "Hmm, if I put this here, then you could..."
- Let kids modify the rules. If they invent a variant, let them try it. Some of the best math happens when kids break and rebuild game rules.
- For 9-15 kids: Set up 4-5 game stations (pairs) or 3-4 stations (groups of 3-4). Have an extra activity for kids who finish a round and are waiting.

> **Zvonkin principle at work**: *Fun is non-negotiable.* If kids don't want to play the game, something is wrong with the game, not the kids. Swap it out. Have a backup game ready every week.

---

### 5. Cool-Down & Journal (5 min)

**What happens**: Gather back to the circle. Ring the bell again (bookend ritual). Each kid shares one thing: something they noticed, something they wondered, something that surprised them, or something that was hard.

**Facilitation notes**:
- Use a talking object (a "math rock," a stuffed animal, a special pencil). Only the holder speaks.
- Model the format: "I noticed that when I flipped the mirror, the letter B looked like a D. I wonder if all letters do that."
- Don't force sharing. "Pass" is always allowed.
- The leader keeps a journal. Not for the kids -- for YOU. Write down what happened, what kids said, what worked, what flopped, what surprised you. Do this during the session or immediately after. (*Zvonkin principle: Journal everything.*)

**What to record in the leader journal**:
- Date, topic, number of kids present.
- Which activities you actually did (vs. planned).
- Direct quotes from kids -- especially wrong answers, creative detours, and "aha" moments.
- What to adjust for next time.
- Which kids struggled, which kids need more challenge.

> **Zvonkin principle at work**: *Journal everything.* Zvonkin's entire book exists because he kept meticulous session notes. Your journal is your most valuable teaching tool. It tells you what to do next week.

---

### 6. Chromebook Bonus (5 min)

**What happens**: Show the week's recommended browser game on a projector or shared screen. Kids will play it independently later (e.g., during free time, at home, or during a designated Chromebook session). This is not instruction time -- it's a quick demo and a "hey, check this out."

**Facilitation notes**:
- Have the game bookmarked and loaded before the session.
- Play one round together on the projector. Let a kid drive.
- Give the URL or show kids where to find it on their bookmarks bar.
- Keep it to 5 minutes. If kids are excited, that's good -- they'll play it later.
- If you're running behind, skip this segment entirely. It's the most expendable.

## Managing 9-15 Kids: Practical Tips

### Room Setup
- **Tables, not rows.** Groups of 3-4 at tables. Kids need flat surfaces for manipulatives.
- **Circle area.** A rug or open floor space for Opening Bell and Cool-Down. Kids sit in a circle, not facing a board.
- **Materials station.** One table or shelf with all session materials pre-sorted into bags or trays. Kids can grab and return on their own.

### Grouping
- **9 kids**: 3 tables of 3, or 4-5 pairs for games.
- **12 kids**: 4 tables of 3, or 6 pairs.
- **15 kids**: 5 tables of 3, or 3 tables of 5 (but 5 is crowded -- prefer pairs within the table).
- **Rotate groups every 2-3 weeks.** Kids get comfortable, then need new partners to shake up their thinking.

### Energy Management
- **High-energy moments**: Game / Play, partner challenges, anything competitive.
- **Low-energy moments**: Opening Bell, Cool-Down, journal.
- **The arc**: Calm start, build up, peak at the Game, wind down. If the energy spikes during Activity 1, ride it. Don't suppress excitement about math.
- **Noise**: Math circles are louder than regular class. That's OK. Worried kids are doing real work? Walk over and listen. If they're talking about the math (even loudly), leave them alone.

### The "I'm Done" Problem
Every group has kids who finish fast. Prepare in advance:
- Extension questions on index cards ("You solved it for 3. Can you solve it for 5?").
- A "challenge corner" with puzzles from previous weeks.
- Let fast finishers become helpers -- but only if they ask questions, not give answers.

### The "I'm Lost" Problem
- Pair struggling kids with a mid-level peer (not the fastest kid).
- Simplify: "Try it with 2 instead of 5."
- Offer a different entry point: "Instead of building it, can you draw it?"
- Never single out. Never say "this group needs extra help." Just quietly sit with them and ask questions.

## Zvonkin's Core Principles -- Quick Reference

Post these on the wall (for you, not the kids):

1. **Questions > Answers.** Pose questions, let kids wrestle. Don't rush to correct.
2. **No explaining away.** Don't use adult authority to impose "correct" answers. A kid who believes something wrong but is thinking hard is in a better place than a kid who parrots the right answer.
3. **Discovery is the goal.** We're not covering curriculum. We want modest but independent discoveries.
4. **Manipulatives matter.** Physical objects make abstract ideas concrete. If kids aren't touching something, rethink the activity.
5. **Fun is non-negotiable.** Intellectual joy and aesthetic pleasure. If it's not fun, it's not working.
6. **Vary the packaging.** Same math concept, different stories and contexts. Symmetry with mirrors on Tuesday, symmetry with paper folding on Thursday.
7. **Journal everything.** Keep a session journal. Write down what actually happened, not what you planned.
8. **Let chaos happen.** Kids will go off-script. That's often where the best math happens. Follow their detours when you can.

## Session Planning Checklist

Use this before each session:

- [ ] Core topic chosen, two activities planned (see `activity-bank.md`)
- [ ] Game selected and tested (play it yourself first)
- [ ] All manipulatives/materials gathered and sorted into table trays
- [ ] Warm-up puzzle written down (don't improvise -- have it ready)
- [ ] Extension questions prepared on index cards for fast finishers
- [ ] Chromebook game bookmarked and tested
- [ ] Leader journal from last session reviewed (what to follow up on?)
- [ ] Backup activity ready in case something flops

---

## A Note on Assessment

There are no grades, no tests, no right/wrong scores. The "assessment" is your journal. Over 10 weeks, you should be able to look back and see:
- Which kids are developing mathematical reasoning (asking "why," making conjectures, testing ideas).
- Which kids are gaining confidence (volunteering answers, trying harder problems).
- Which topics landed and which didn't.

If a kid leaves math circle thinking "math is fun and I'm the kind of person who can figure things out," you've succeeded. Everything else is bonus.

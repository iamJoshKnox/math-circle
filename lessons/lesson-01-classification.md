# Lesson 1: "What is a Math Circle?" — Odd One Out & Classification

## Theme
Introduction to Math Circle culture. Kids learn that math is about *noticing*, *arguing*, and *wondering* — not racing to answers. Classification (sorting by properties) is the vehicle.

## Learning Goals
- Establish Math Circle norms: listen, disagree respectfully, explain your thinking
- Discover that objects can be grouped in many valid ways
- Practice articulating *why* something does or doesn't belong
- Experience a Venn diagram as a physical, embodied structure

## Timed Script

### Opening Bell (5 min) — "Welcome to Math Circle"

**[0:00–1:00] Gathering Ritual**

> "Welcome to Math Circle! My name is ___. In Math Circle we do something different from regular math class. Here, there are no grades, no tests, and sometimes no right answers. Our job is to *think hard* and *explain why*."

Hand out name tags. Kids sit in a circle on the floor or around a table cluster.

**[1:00–3:00] Norm Setting**

> "We have three rules in Math Circle:
> 1. **Listen** when someone is talking.
> 2. **Disagree with ideas, not people.** You can say 'I think differently because...'
> 3. **It's okay to be wrong.** Wrong guesses help everyone learn."

Hold up three fingers. Have kids repeat each rule back.

**[3:00–5:00] Warm-Up Number Talk**

> "Quick puzzle to wake up our brains. I'm thinking of something in this room that there are exactly four of. What could it be?"

*Anticipated kid responses:*
- "Legs on a chair!" (Good — confirm and ask for another)
- "Walls!" (Great noticing)
- "Windows!" (May or may not be 4 — let them count and debate)

*Teacher move:* Accept all answers. If a kid says something wrong, say "Hmm, let's check — can we count?" Do NOT correct; let the group self-correct.

---

### Main Activity 1 (15 min) — Odd One Out

**[5:00–7:00] Introduction**

Hold up the first Odd One Out card (4 images, e.g., dog, cat, fish, car).

> "Look at these four pictures. Which one doesn't belong? ... But here's the trick: I think *any* of them could be the odd one out. Your job is to explain *why* your choice doesn't belong."

*Key teacher move:* Do NOT say which one is "right." Every justified answer is valid.

**[7:00–12:00] Guided Practice (Whole Group, 3 cards)**

Show cards one at a time. For each card:

1. Give 20 seconds of silent think time.
2. Ask: "Who has an idea? Which one doesn't belong, and why?"
3. After one answer, ask: "Does anyone have a *different* odd one out?"
4. Push for justification: "What property are you using to sort them?"

*Card example — Circle, Square, Triangle, Red Circle:*

*Anticipated responses:*
- "The square, because the others are round." → "Interesting — are the triangle and circle both round?"
- "The red circle, because it's a different color." → "So you're sorting by *color*. Nice."
- "The triangle, because it has three sides and the others have four or zero." → "Whoa, zero sides — what do we think about that?"

*Teacher moves:*
- Use wait time (count silently to 5 before calling on anyone).
- If kids shout out, gently redirect: "I love the energy — let's give everyone think time."
- Revoice: "So Mia is saying the triangle is odd because of its number of sides. Did I get that right, Mia?"

**[12:00–20:00] Partner Practice (4–5 more cards)**

- Pair kids up. Give each pair 2 cards.
- Each partner picks a different odd one out and explains to the other.
- Circulate and listen. Note interesting justifications to share later.

> "Talk to your partner. Can you find TWO different odd ones out on the same card?"

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Challenge pair to find a reason for ALL FOUR to be the odd one out.
- **Too hard:** Sit with the pair. Point to one image. "How is this one different from the others? What do you notice?"

---

### Main Activity 2 (15 min) — Sorting & Venn Diagrams

**[20:00–25:00] Sorting Introduction**

Dump the bucket of mixed objects onto a central table or floor area.

> "Now we're going to sort. I'll give you a rule, and you put objects where they belong."

Place two hula hoops on the floor, not overlapping. Label one "RED" and one "HAS CORNERS."

> "If an object is red, it goes in this circle. If it has corners, it goes in that circle. If it's neither, it stays outside. Ready? Each person grabs one object, decides where it goes, and places it."

Kids take turns. After several objects:

> "Wait — what about this red block? It's red AND it has corners. Where does it go?"

*Anticipated responses:*
- "Put it in both!" → "But it's one object — can it be in two places?"
- "Put it in the middle!" → "Hmm, there is no middle right now..."
- Silence / confusion → Good! This is the discovery moment.

**[25:00–28:00] Inventing the Venn Diagram**

*Teacher move:* Slowly slide the two hoops so they overlap.

> "What if we did this? Now there's a space that's inside BOTH circles. What goes there?"

Let kids re-sort. They should move red-cornered objects to the overlap.

> "This thing we just made has a name — it's called a Venn diagram. But you basically invented it just now."

**[28:00–35:00] Kid-Chosen Categories**

> "Now YOU pick the rules. Who has an idea for two categories?"

*Anticipated category suggestions:*
- "Big and small" → Works, but ask "How big is big? Let's decide."
- "Animals and not animals" → Fine but won't overlap much. Steer toward a combo.
- "Soft and blue" → Great, will generate overlaps.

Run 2 rounds with kid-chosen categories. Let debates happen ("Is this blue or purple?"). These debates ARE the math.

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Introduce a THIRD hula hoop for a three-way Venn diagram. "Where does something go if it fits all three?"
- **Too hard:** Reduce to one hoop: "Inside = has the property. Outside = doesn't." Build up to two.

---

### Game / Play (15 min) — "Guess My Rule"

**[35:00–37:00] Setup**

> "Now we play a game. I'm thinking of a secret rule. I'll sort some objects, and you have to figure out my rule."

**[37:00–45:00] Teacher Leads 2 Rounds**

*Round 1:* Secretly sort by "made of wood" vs. "not made of wood." Place objects one at a time into two groups. After 4–5 objects:

> "Does anyone think they know my rule? Don't say it out loud yet — instead, hand me an object and tell me which group it goes in. If you're right, you might know the rule."

*Anticipated responses:*
- "Brown things!" → Place a brown plastic item in "not wood." "Hmm, still think it's brown things?"
- "Hard things?" → Place a hard metal key in "not wood." "Interesting..."
- "Wood!" → "Test it. Hand me something."

*Teacher move:* Never confirm the rule verbally until at least 5 kids have successfully sorted an object. Then ask a kid to state it.

*Round 2:* Sort by "has a hole in it" (buttons, keys, beads vs. blocks, crayons, coins).

**[45:00–50:00] Kids Lead**

Ask for a volunteer to be the sorter. The rest of the group guesses. Run 1–2 kid-led rounds.

*Teacher move:* If the kid-leader's rule is too easy (e.g., "blue"), praise them and suggest: "Can you think of a trickier rule next time?" If too hard or inconsistent, quietly help them refine.

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Allow two-property rules ("things that are red AND small").
- **Too hard:** Use yes/no — "Does this go in Group A? Yes or no?"

---

### Cool-Down & Journal (5 min)

**[50:00–53:00] Share Circle**

> "Before we finish, let's go around. Tell me one thing you *noticed* or *wondered* today. There's no wrong answer."

*Anticipated responses:*
- "I noticed that things can belong to two groups."
- "I wondered why the Venn diagram has that middle part."
- "I noticed that everyone had different odd ones out."

*Teacher move:* If a kid says "I don't know," offer a scaffold: "What was your favorite part? What surprised you?"

**[53:00–55:00] Journal Time**

> "Open your Math Circle journal. On the first page, write or draw one thing from today. It can be a picture of the Venn diagram, a favorite odd-one-out card, your name — whatever you want."

Give kids 2 minutes. Collect journals (or have a storage spot in the room). Journals stay in the classroom.

---

### Chromebook Bonus (5 min)

**[55:00–60:00] Introduce the Week's Browser Game**

> "This week's Chromebook game is called **Odd One Out**. Let me show you."

**Game Description:** A browser-based "Which One Doesn't Belong?" game. Each screen shows 4 images. The player clicks one and types or selects a reason. Multiple correct answers are accepted. Levels progress from concrete objects to shapes to numbers.

*Suggested resource:* [Which One Doesn't Belong?](https://wodb.ca/) — free, no login, works on Chromebooks.

> "You can play this during free Chromebook time this week. See how many different reasons you can find for EACH card. Try to find a reason for all four!"

## Differentiation Summary

| Challenge Level | Adjustments |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Needs support** | Reduce choices to 3 instead of 4. Use concrete objects instead of pictures. Pair with a stronger peer. Use physical pointing instead of verbal explanation. |
| **On target** | Standard activities as described. Encourage multiple valid answers. |
| **Needs challenge** | Find ALL FOUR odd-one-out reasons per card. Three-circle Venn diagrams. Create own Odd One Out cards for classmates. Two-property sorting rules. |

## Zvonkin Principles in This Lesson

| Principle | Where It Appears |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Questions > Answers | "Which one doesn't belong?" has no single right answer |
| No explaining away | Teacher never declares the "correct" odd one out |
| Discovery is the goal | Kids discover the Venn overlap themselves |
| Manipulatives matter | Physical objects, hula hoops, hands-on sorting |
| Fun is non-negotiable | Guess My Rule is a genuine game with suspense |
| Vary the packaging | Same concept (classification) delivered as cards, physical sorting, and a guessing game |
| Journal everything | Cool-down journal reflection |
| Let chaos happen | Debates about categories ("Is this blue or purple?") are encouraged |

## Appendix A: Odd One Out Card Suggestions

1. **Dog, Cat, Fish, Car** — Animal/not-animal; legs/no legs; fur/no fur
2. **Circle, Square, Triangle, Red Circle** — Shape, color, number of sides
3. **Apple, Banana, Carrot, Orange** — Fruit/vegetable; color; shape
4. **Bicycle, Motorcycle, Scooter, Airplane** — Wheels; flies; engine/no engine
5. **2, 5, 10, 11** — Even/odd; number of digits; divisibility
6. **Book, Newspaper, Tablet, Shoe** — Reading material; has screen; wearable
7. **Snowflake, Star, Arrow, Heart** — Symmetry; pointy; natural/human-made
8. **Guitar, Drum, Piano, Flute** — Strings; size; how you play it

# Lesson 2: Symmetry & Mirrors

## Theme
Kids discover symmetry through their hands, pegboards, ink blots, and paper cutting. The mirror is the verification tool — not the teacher.

## Learning Goals
- Recognize line symmetry in shapes, objects, and letters
- Create symmetric designs on pegboards and paper
- Use a physical mirror to *verify* symmetry rather than relying on an authority figure
- Experience that some things have MORE than one line of symmetry

## Timed Script

### Opening Bell (5 min) — "Mirror, Mirror"

**[0:00–1:30] Gathering Ritual**

Kids sit in a circle. Place a mirror face-down in the center.

> "Welcome back to Math Circle! Last week we sorted and classified. Today we're going to discover something that's all around us — in nature, in buildings, even in your own face. But first, a warm-up."

**[1:30–5:00] Number Talk: Symmetry of Hands**

> "Hold up both hands, palms facing you. What do you notice?"

*Anticipated responses:*
- "They look the same!" → "Do they? Are they exactly the same?"
- "They're backwards of each other." → "Interesting — say more about that."
- "They're like a mirror." → "Ooh, mirror. Hold that thought."

> "Put your palms together. Now slowly open them like a book. See how your left hand mirrors your right? That 'mirror-ness' has a math name: **symmetry**."

*Teacher move:* Don't over-define. Let the word settle. They'll build the meaning through activity.

> "Today's big question: **How can you tell if something is symmetric?**"

---

### Main Activity 1 (15 min) — Pegboard Symmetry

**[5:00–8:00] Setup and Demo**

Hand out geoboards (or dot-grid paper) and rubber bands. Hold up your geoboard.

> "Watch. I'm going to put a rubber band on this side of my board."

Stretch a rubber band to make a simple shape on the LEFT half of the geoboard — e.g., a triangle using 3 pegs.

> "Now pretend there's a mirror running right down the middle of the board, top to bottom."

Run your finger down the center column.

> "Your job: make the OTHER side look like the mirror reflection. So the whole thing is symmetric."

**[8:00–15:00] Kids Work (Individual)**

Give kids 3 challenges, escalating in difficulty:

**Challenge 1:** Teacher makes a simple shape on the left (L-shape using 3 pegs). Kids mirror it on the right.

**Challenge 2:** Teacher makes a more complex shape (staircase using 5 pegs). Kids mirror it.

**Challenge 3:** Kids create their OWN design on one half, then swap boards with a partner. The partner completes the mirror image.

*Key questions while circulating:*
- "How did you figure out where that peg goes?"
- "Is the mirror-peg the same distance from the middle line?"
- "What happens to something that's ON the middle line?"

*Anticipated struggles:*
- Diagonal lines are hardest to mirror. Kids often just copy instead of reflecting.
- *Teacher move:* Hand the kid a mirror. "Hold this on the center line. What do you see? Now make it match."

**[15:00–20:00] Verification with Mirrors**

> "Now here's the cool part. Take your mirror and stand it up right on the center line of your board. Look into the mirror. Does the reflection match what you built on the other side?"

Let kids check their own work and their partner's.

> "The mirror doesn't lie! If it matches, your design is symmetric. If it doesn't, what went wrong?"

*Teacher move:* If a kid's design doesn't match, do NOT fix it for them. Ask: "Which peg is in the wrong spot? How can you tell?" Let the mirror be the authority.

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Use a HORIZONTAL mirror line instead of vertical. Or use a diagonal mirror line (very challenging).
- **Too hard:** Use only 2–3 pegs. Reduce to simple horizontal or vertical lines. Work one-on-one with the mirror.

### Main Activity 2 (15 min) — Ink Blots & Snowflakes

**[20:00–28:00] Ink-Blot Symmetry**

> "Now we're going to make symmetry with paint. This is going to be a little messy — and that's okay."

**Procedure:**
1. Each kid folds a white sheet of paper in half, then opens it back up.
2. On ONE side of the fold, they drop/dab 3–4 blobs of paint. Not too much.
3. They fold the paper closed and press firmly, rubbing gently.
4. Open it up.

> "What happened? What do you see?"

*Anticipated responses:*
- "It's a butterfly!" → "What makes it look like a butterfly?"
- "Both sides are the same!" → "Are they *exactly* the same? Check with your mirror."
- "It's symmetric!" → "How do you know? Where's the line of symmetry?"

*Key question:*
> "Why did this happen? Why are both sides the same?"

*Anticipated explanation attempts:*
- "Because the paint squished to the other side." → "Right — the fold acted like a mirror line."

*Teacher move:* If kids get excited about the art (they will), let them enjoy it for a moment. Then redirect: "Beautiful! Now — mathematician hat on — where exactly is the line of symmetry?"

**[28:00–35:00] Snowflake Cutting**

> "Now let's make symmetry with scissors. Has anyone made paper snowflakes before?"

**Procedure:**
1. Give each kid a coffee filter or square piece of paper.
2. Fold in half. Fold in half again. Fold diagonally once more (so it's folded in thirds or quarters).
3. Cut small shapes along the edges — triangles, half-circles, notches.
4. Unfold carefully.

> "Before you open it — PREDICT. What do you think it will look like? Tell your neighbor."

Kids unfold.

> "Count the lines of symmetry in your snowflake. How many does it have?"

*Anticipated responses:*
- "Two!" or "Four!" → Both may be right depending on folds.
- "It's the same everywhere!" → "That's a LOT of symmetry. More than our pegboard designs?"

*Key question:*
> "Why does folding and cutting create symmetry? What does the fold DO?"

*Teacher move:* Connect back to the ink blot. "The fold is like the mirror line. When you cut through multiple layers, every layer gets the same cut."

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Challenge: "Can you make a snowflake with EXACTLY two lines of symmetry? Exactly one?"
- **Too hard:** Pre-fold the paper for the child. Mark dots where they should cut. Let them cut and experience the magic of unfolding.

---

### Game / Play (15 min) — "Symmetric Letters & Mirror Challenge"

**[35:00–42:00] Which Letters Are Symmetric?**

Spread the large letter cards on the floor: A, B, C, D, H, M, T, S, Z, J.

> "Which of these letters have a line of symmetry? Use your mirror to test them."

Kids grab mirrors and test letters.

*Key discoveries:*
- A has a vertical line of symmetry
- B has a horizontal line of symmetry (the printed version — some kids will debate this)
- H has BOTH vertical and horizontal
- S and Z have rotational symmetry but NO line symmetry (this will blow minds)
- J has no symmetry

> "Some letters have vertical symmetry, some have horizontal, some have both, and some have NONE. Can you sort them into groups?"

*Teacher move:* Let the debates happen. "Is B really symmetric? Let's check with the mirror." The mirror settles arguments — not the teacher.

**[42:00–50:00] Mirror Challenge Pairs**

Pair kids up. Give each pair one mirror, standing upright between them.

> "Partner A: draw a simple shape or pattern on your paper — but ONLY on your side of the mirror. Partner B: look in the mirror and try to draw what you see on YOUR paper. Then compare!"

After 3 minutes, switch roles.

*Variation:* Partner A builds with small blocks on their side; Partner B mirrors the build on their side. Check by looking in the mirror.

*Teacher move:* Celebrate mismatches as much as matches. "Ooh, what went different? Why is mirrors-drawing so tricky?"

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Draw a complex figure. Or try to draw the mirror image WITHOUT looking in the mirror, then check.
- **Too hard:** Use only 2–3 blocks. Keep shapes very simple (a straight line of blocks).

---

### Cool-Down & Journal (5 min)

**[50:00–53:00] Share Circle**

> "We're going to go around the circle. Tell me: where have you seen symmetry OUTSIDE of this room? In real life?"

*Anticipated responses:*
- "Butterflies!" → "Yes! What's the line of symmetry on a butterfly?"
- "My face." → "Is your face PERFECTLY symmetric? Hmm..."
- "Buildings." → "Which part of a building?"
- "The letter in my name!" → "Which letter? Does your whole name have symmetry?"

**[53:00–55:00] Journal Time**

> "In your journal, draw something symmetric. It could be your ink blot, your snowflake, a letter, or something you make up. Draw the line of symmetry with a dotted line."

### Chromebook Bonus (5 min)

**[55:00–60:00] Introduce the Week's Browser Game**

> "This week's Chromebook game is called **Symmetry Mirror**. Let me show you."

**Game Description:** A browser-based symmetry game where one half of a picture or pattern is shown, and the player completes the other half by clicking squares on a grid. A virtual mirror line runs down the center. Some levels use vertical symmetry; harder levels use horizontal or diagonal. The game checks the player's work by "folding" the image.

## Differentiation Summary

| Challenge Level | Adjustments |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Needs support** | Fewer pegs on geoboard (2–3). Pre-folded paper. Mirror as constant companion. One-on-one support. Tracing over dotted symmetric outlines. |
| **On target** | Standard activities as described. Partner work for accountability. |
| **Needs challenge** | Diagonal mirror lines on pegboard. Snowflakes with specific symmetry counts. Predict mirror image before checking. Letters → full words: is "MOM" symmetric? "WOW"? |

## Zvonkin Principles in This Lesson

| Principle | Where It Appears |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Questions > Answers | "How can you tell if something is symmetric?" drives the whole session |
| No explaining away | Mirror verifies — teacher does not declare right/wrong |
| Discovery is the goal | Kids discover the Venn-diagram-like fold = mirror line connection |
| Manipulatives matter | Pegboards, mirrors, paint, scissors, paper |
| Fun is non-negotiable | Ink blots are delightful. Snowflake cutting is magical. |
| Vary the packaging | Same concept (symmetry) via pegboard, paint, cutting, letters, drawing |
| Journal everything | Draw a symmetric design with line marked |
| Let chaos happen | Debates about letters (Is B symmetric? Is S?), messy paint, imperfect snowflakes |

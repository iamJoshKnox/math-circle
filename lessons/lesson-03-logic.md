# Lesson 3: Logic Puzzles

## Theme
Kids encounter pure logical reasoning through nonsense-word puzzles, constraint problems, and riddles. The lesson emphasizes that logic works even when the content is silly or unfamiliar — you don't need to "know things" to reason well.

## Learning Goals
- Practice drawing conclusions from given statements (deductive reasoning)
- Distinguish between what you KNOW from clues vs. what you're GUESSING
- Experience that logical reasoning works even with made-up words
- Build confidence in saying "I don't have enough information to know"

## Timed Script

### Opening Bell (5 min) — "True or False... or Can't Tell?"

**[0:00–1:00] Gathering Ritual**

> "Welcome back to Math Circle! Today we are going to be detectives. Not regular detectives — LOGIC detectives. Our clues might sound weird. Our characters have silly names. But the thinking is real."

**[1:00–5:00] Warm-Up: True, False, or Can't Tell?**

> "I'm going to say some statements. For each one, show me: thumbs up if it's TRUE, thumbs down if it's FALSE, and hand flat if you CAN'T TELL."

Read each statement. Wait for all thumbs before discussing.

1. "Every kid in this room is a person." → TRUE (thumbs up)
2. "Every person in the world is a kid." → FALSE (thumbs down)
3. "Everyone in this room has a pet." → CAN'T TELL (hand flat — maybe, maybe not)
4. "If it's raining outside, the ground is wet." → TRUE (most will agree)
5. "The ground is wet. So it must be raining." → CAN'T TELL! (This is the key one.)

*Anticipated responses to #5:*
- "True! If the ground is wet, it rained!" → "Always? What else could make the ground wet?"
- "Someone could have sprayed a hose!" → "Exactly. Wet ground doesn't PROVE rain."
- Confusion → Good! This is the lesson in miniature.

*Teacher move:* Spend a moment on #5. This is the difference between "if A then B" and "if B then A." Don't use that language — just let them feel the difference.

> "That last one is tricky. In logic, you can't go backwards like that. Today we'll do more tricky thinking like this."

### Main Activity 1 (15 min) — "Who Is Booter?"

**[5:00–10:00] Meet Gobr and Stoon**

Hold up the two character cards — one for Gobr (maybe a green blobby monster) and one for Stoon (maybe a spiky purple creature).

> "Meet Gobr and Stoon. They live on Planet Zibble. On Planet Zibble, they use different words than we do. Today, we're going to figure out what those words mean — using only CLUES."

Write on the board: **GOBR** and **STOON** with the pictures.

> "Here's the question: **Who is booter — Gobr or Stoon?** But wait... we don't even know what 'booter' means yet! We need clues."

**[10:00–20:00] Clue-by-Clue Deduction**

Reveal clues ONE AT A TIME. After each clue, ask the group what they can figure out.

**Clue 1:** "Gobr is taller than Stoon."

> "What do we know now? Can we figure out who is booter?"

*Anticipated:* "No! We don't know what booter means."

*Teacher move:* "Right. We need more information. Let's get another clue."

**Clue 2:** "On Planet Zibble, 'booter' means 'the taller one.'"

> "NOW can we figure it out? Who is booter?"

*Anticipated:* "Gobr! Because Gobr is taller!" → "How do you know?" → "Clue 1 says Gobr is taller, and Clue 2 says booter means taller."

> "Excellent. You just used TWO clues together to figure something out. That's called a DEDUCTION."

**Round 2 — Harder:**

Clear the board. New question: **"Who is snig — Gobr or Stoon?"**

**Clue 1:** "Gobr has 3 eyes. Stoon has 5 eyes."
**Clue 2:** "The one who is snig has more eyes."

> "Who is snig?"

*Anticipated:* "Stoon! Because 5 is more than 3." → "Show me how you know. Point to the clues."

**Round 3 — Trickiest:**

New question: **"Who is plonk?"**

**Clue 1:** "Gobr eats zibberberries. Stoon eats moonrocks."
**Clue 2:** "Plonk means 'the one who can fly.'"

> "Who is plonk?"

*Anticipated:*
- "Gobr!" or "Stoon!" → "Wait — which clue tells us who can fly?"
- Silence / confusion → "Hmm. Do we have enough information?"
- "We CAN'T TELL!" → "YES! Why can't we tell?"

*Teacher move:* This is the crucial moment. Celebrate the kids who recognize that the clues don't give enough information. This is harder than solving — it's recognizing unsolvability.

> "Sometimes in logic, the answer is 'we don't have enough information.' And that's a GREAT answer. Real mathematicians say that all the time."

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Add 3-clue chains: "The plonk is heavier than the snig. Gobr is heavier than Stoon. Who is the plonk?"
- **Too hard:** Use only 2 characters and 1 property. Physically act it out with two kids playing Gobr and Stoon.

### Main Activity 2 (15 min) — Construction Foreman

**[20:00–25:00] Setup**

> "Now a different kind of logic puzzle. This one is about building a team."

Read the scenario aloud, slowly, while displaying it on the board:

> "A construction foreman needs to pick a team to build a house. There are four workers: **Anna, Ben, Clara, and Dan**. But there are RULES about who can work together."

Write the rules on the board one at a time:

**Rule 1:** Anna and Ben cannot work together. (They argue too much.)
**Rule 2:** If Clara works, then Dan must work too. (Clara needs Dan's help.)
**Rule 3:** The team needs at least 2 workers.

> "The foreman wants to hire Clara. Who else MUST be on the team? Who CAN'T be? Who MIGHT be?"

**[25:00–32:00] Group Reasoning**

Let kids think, then discuss as a group.

*Scaffolded questions:*

1. "If Clara works, what does Rule 2 tell us?"
   - *Expected:* "Dan has to work too." → Write: Team so far = {Clara, Dan}

2. "Can Anna also be on the team?"
   - *Expected:* "Yes... unless Ben is also there." → "So Anna is POSSIBLE."

3. "Can Ben also be on the team?"
   - *Expected:* "Yes... unless Anna is also there." → "So Ben is POSSIBLE too."

4. "Can both Anna AND Ben be on the team?"
   - *Expected:* "No! Rule 1." → "Good."

5. "So what are the possible teams?"
   - {Clara, Dan} — satisfies Rule 3? Yes (2 workers). Valid.
   - {Clara, Dan, Anna} — Anna and Ben not both present. Valid.
   - {Clara, Dan, Ben} — Anna and Ben not both present. Valid.
   - {Clara, Dan, Anna, Ben} — INVALID, Rule 1.

*Teacher move:* Write out the possible teams. Let kids check each one against ALL rules.

**[32:00–35:00] Variation**

> "New question: What if the foreman wants Anna on the team but NOT Clara? What teams are possible?"

- If not Clara, Rule 2 about Dan is irrelevant (it says IF Clara, THEN Dan — but if Clara isn't there, Dan can be or not be).
- Anna is on team. Ben can't be (Rule 1). So: {Anna, Dan} or {Anna} — but {Anna} fails Rule 3.
- Valid teams: {Anna, Dan}, {Anna, Dan, ...but no Ben, no Clara}.

*Wait — can we have just {Anna, Dan}? Yes! Rule 1 only blocks Anna+Ben. Rule 2 only applies if Clara is included.*

Let kids work through this. It's okay if they don't all get it — the reasoning PROCESS is the goal.

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Add a 4th rule: "Dan won't work on Fridays. The house must be built on a Friday. Now what?"
- **Too hard:** Reduce to 3 workers and 2 rules. Use physical name cards that kids can move in and out of a "team" area. Dramatically act out the arguments between Anna and Ben.

### Game / Play (15 min) — Riddle Round Robin

**[35:00–38:00] Introduction**

> "Now let's play a riddle game. I'll read riddles, and you think about them. Some are tricky. Remember: in Math Circle, the THINKING is more important than the answer."

**[38:00–50:00] Riddles**

Read riddles one at a time. Give think time. Discuss as a group.

**Riddle 1 (Warm-up):**
> "I have cities, but no houses. I have mountains, but no trees. I have water, but no fish. What am I?"

*Answer: A map.*
*Teacher move:* After the answer, ask "How did you figure it out? What was the key clue?"

**Riddle 2 (Logic):**
> "There are three boxes. One has only apples, one has only oranges, and one has both. Every box is labeled WRONG. The box labeled 'Apples' doesn't have only apples. The box labeled 'Oranges' doesn't have only oranges. The box labeled 'Both' doesn't have both. You can pick ONE fruit from ONE box (without looking). Which box do you pick from to figure out ALL the labels?"

*Answer: Pick from the box labeled "Both." Since it's wrong, it has only apples or only oranges. Whichever you pull out, you know what that box is. Then you can deduce the rest because the remaining labels are also wrong.*

*Teacher move:* This is hard for 2nd graders. Walk through it slowly with physical boxes if possible. It's okay if only 1–2 kids follow the full logic. The others absorb the APPROACH.

**Riddle 3 (Lateral thinking):**
> "A rooster lays an egg on the very top of a roof. Which side does the egg roll down — left or right?"

*Answer: Roosters don't lay eggs!*
*Anticipated:* Lots of guessing "left" or "right" before someone catches it. Huge laughs.

**Riddle 4 (Deduction):**
> "Sara is taller than Max. Max is taller than Lily. Is Sara taller or shorter than Lily?"

*Answer: Taller.*
*Teacher move:* Have three kids stand up and physically arrange themselves to verify.

**Riddle 5 (If time):**
> "You have 8 coins that look identical. One is slightly heavier. You have a balance scale. What is the smallest number of weighings needed to find the heavy coin?"

*Answer: 2 weighings.* (Weigh 3 vs 3. If balanced, weigh the remaining 2. If unbalanced, take the heavy group of 3, weigh 1 vs 1.)

*Teacher move:* This is very advanced. Use it only if the group is flying. Use physical objects (blocks) and a real or pretend balance if possible. It's fine if they don't solve it — plant the seed.

**Additional riddles if time allows:**

6. "What has hands but can't clap?" (A clock)
7. "I'm an odd number. Take away one letter and I become even. What am I?" (Seven → even)
8. "A farmer has 17 sheep. All but 9 run away. How many are left?" (9)

*Differentiation:*
- **Too easy:** Let fast-solving kids make up their OWN riddles for the group.
- **Too hard:** Do more riddles like #1 and #3 (pattern/lateral thinking) and fewer like #2 and #5 (multi-step deduction). Give generous hints: "Think about the WORDS in the riddle, not just the math."

### Cool-Down & Journal (5 min)

**[50:00–53:00] Share Circle**

> "What was the hardest thing about today's logic puzzles? What made your brain work the most?"

*Anticipated responses:*
- "The one where we couldn't figure it out!" (plonk) → "Yes! Knowing when you CAN'T solve something is a skill."
- "The box riddle was hard." → "What made it hard?"
- "I liked the roosters one because it was a trick!" → "Why do you think trick questions exist in logic?"

**[53:00–55:00] Journal Time**

> "In your journal, write or draw your favorite riddle or puzzle from today. If you want, make up your OWN riddle and we'll try it next time."

## Differentiation Summary

| Challenge Level | Adjustments |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Needs support** | Fewer characters/properties in Gobr & Stoon. Physical acting out of scenarios. Visual clue cards instead of verbal. More lateral-thinking riddles, fewer multi-step deductions. |
| **On target** | Standard activities as described. Encourage "can't tell" as a valid answer. Partner discussion before whole-group sharing. |
| **Needs challenge** | 3-clue chains. Construction foreman with 4+ rules. Balance-scale puzzles. Invent own logic puzzles. Explore "the converse isn't always true" with more examples. |

## Zvonkin Principles in This Lesson

| Principle | Where It Appears |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Questions > Answers | "Who is plonk?" intentionally has no answer — the question IS the point |
| No explaining away | Teacher never reveals the "trick" in riddles before kids wrestle with it |
| Discovery is the goal | Kids discover that "can't tell" is a valid logical conclusion |
| Manipulatives matter | Character cards, physical boxes, kids standing up to check height comparisons |
| Fun is non-negotiable | Gobr and Stoon are ridiculous. Rooster riddle gets big laughs. |
| Vary the packaging | Logic via aliens, construction workers, and riddles — same reasoning, different skins |
| Journal everything | Kids write favorite riddle or invent their own |
| Let chaos happen | Multiple kids shouting different guesses during riddles; debates about plonk |

## Appendix A: Gobr & Stoon Clue Sets

**Set 1 (Easy):**
- Clue: Gobr is taller. Booter = taller one. → Gobr is booter.

**Set 2 (Medium):**
- Clue: Gobr has 3 eyes, Stoon has 5. Snig = more eyes. → Stoon is snig.

**Set 3 (Impossible):**
- Clue: Gobr eats zibberberries, Stoon eats moonrocks. Plonk = can fly. → Can't tell!

**Set 4 (Chain):**
- Clue: Gobr is faster than Stoon. The zapper is faster than the blink. Gobr is the zapper. → So Gobr is faster (confirmed by both clues). Who is the blink? Stoon.

**Set 5 (Negation):**
- Clue: Stoon is NOT the gruzzle. There are only two creatures. → Gobr is the gruzzle.

# Lesson 9: Spatial Reasoning & Maps

## Theme
Spatial Reasoning & Maps — floor plans, perspective-taking, and building from diagrams

## Learning Goals
- Translate between 3D spaces and 2D representations (floor plans, maps, diagrams)
- Practice perspective-taking: "What does the other person see?"
- Build 3D structures from 2D diagrams and create diagrams from 3D structures
- Develop spatial vocabulary: above, below, behind, next to, left of, right of, bird's eye view
- Experience the challenge and joy of communicating spatial information precisely

## Zvonkin References
- Floor plans: p. 172
- "What does the other person see?": p. 178
- Building from diagrams: p. 270

## Materials Checklist
- [ ] Unifix cubes or wooden blocks (at least 20 per pair of kids)
- [ ] Barrier boards or large books (to hide block structures between partners)
- [ ] Graph paper (large squares, 1-inch grid), 3 sheets per kid
- [ ] Pencils, erasers, rulers
- [ ] Printed "bird's eye view" floor plan of the classroom (prepared in advance — a rough one is fine)
- [ ] Printed building diagrams (front view, side view, top view of simple block structures), 1 set per pair
- [ ] Small toy figures or action figures (to place in floor plans for perspective games)
- [ ] Camera or tablet for taking photos of block structures (optional but great)
- [ ] Whiteboard and markers
- [ ] Math journals
- [ ] Chromebooks with "Floor Plan Builder" game bookmarked

## Timed Script

### Opening Bell (5 min) — "The Bird's Eye Riddle"

**Setup:** Before the session, draw (or print) a simple bird's eye view of the classroom — tables, rug area, door, windows, teacher's desk, bookshelf. Don't label anything. Post it on the board.

**Teacher says:** "I found this strange drawing. It's a picture of a room. Can anyone figure out which room it is?"

**Anticipated responses:**
- Silence, squinting
- "It looks like... rectangles?"
- "Wait — is that the door?"
- "That's THIS room!" (Breakthrough moment)
- "But where are we? Where do we sit?"

**Teacher move:** Once someone identifies it, say: "This is called a FLOOR PLAN. It's what our room looks like if a bird flew above and looked straight down. Now — can you find where YOU sit?"

Let kids come up and point to their seat on the floor plan.

**Key question:** "What's missing from this picture? What can't you see in a bird's eye view?" (Height! You can't tell how tall anything is. You can't see what's on the walls.)

**Teacher says:** "Today we're going to become architects and mapmakers. We'll draw what things look like from above, from the side, and from the front — and we'll see how DIFFERENT the same thing looks depending on where you stand."

---

### Main Activity 1 (15 min) — "What Does the Other Person See?"

**Setup:** Kids sit in pairs across from each other at tables. Place a small arrangement of 3-4 colorful objects in the center of each table (a block tower, a toy figure, a cup, etc.). Give each kid a sheet of paper and pencil.

**Step 1 — Draw what YOU see (5 min):**

**Teacher says:** "Draw EXACTLY what you see from where you're sitting. Don't move. Just draw the objects as they look from YOUR side."

**While kids draw:** Don't correct or guide. Let the drawings be imperfect — that's data.

**Step 2 — Compare (3 min):**

**Teacher says:** "Now show your drawing to your partner. Do your pictures look the same?"

**Anticipated responses:**
- "No! Mine is totally different!"
- "I can see the red block but they can't!"
- "The cup is on the left for me but the right for them!"

**Key question:** "Why are your pictures different? You drew the SAME objects!"

**Teacher move:** This is a profound moment. Let them wrestle with it. The answer: perspective. What you see depends on where you stand.

**Step 3 — Predict and check (7 min):**

**Teacher says:** "Now WITHOUT getting up, try to draw what your PARTNER sees. What does the table look like from THEIR side?"

**Anticipated difficulty:** This is HARD for 7-year-olds. Some will just redraw their own view. Some will mirror it. Some will genuinely try to imagine the other viewpoint.

**Teacher says:** "When you're done, walk over to your partner's side and check. How close were you?"

**Teacher move:** Celebrate the attempts. "You got the cup on the right side — it IS on their right! But the block tower... what happened there?"

**Key question:** "What was the hardest part about imagining someone else's view?"

**Debrief (2 min):** "Mathematicians and architects have to think about this all the time. When they draw a building, they draw it from the front, the side, AND the top. Each view shows different information."

---

### Main Activity 2 (15 min) — "Build It / Draw It"

**Setup:** Pairs of kids. Each pair gets ~20 blocks and a barrier (large book stood upright between them). One kid is the "Builder," the other is the "Describer." Give the Describer a printed building diagram.

**Part A — Build from Description (8 min):**

**Teacher says:** "Describers — you have a picture of a block structure. Your job is to tell your partner how to build it using ONLY WORDS. No pointing, no peeking! Builders — you can ask questions, but you can't look at the picture."

**Printed diagrams include:**
1. Easy: An L-shape made of 4 blocks (front view)
2. Medium: A 2-story tower with a bridge (front view + top view)
3. Hard: A T-shape shown from the side only

**Anticipated challenges:**
- "Put the block on the right." "MY right or YOUR right?"
- "Stack two blocks." "On top of which one?"
- Frustration when the build doesn't match the picture

**Teacher move:** This is where spatial language develops. When kids struggle, don't fix it — ask: "Is there a more specific way to say that?" Encourage terms like "on top of," "to the left of," "in front of."

**After building:** Remove the barrier. Compare the structure to the diagram. Celebrate matches. For mismatches, ask: "Where did the communication break down? What word caused confusion?"

**Part B — Draw from a Build (7 min):**

Switch roles. The new Builder constructs a small structure (5-8 blocks) of their own design. The barrier stays up. The new Describer must now draw the structure from the Builder's verbal description.

**Teacher says:** "Builders, describe your structure so your partner can draw it. Describers, draw what they tell you — front view only."

**After drawing:** Remove the barrier and compare. Did the drawing match?

**Key question for the whole group:** "Was it easier to build from a description or draw from one? Why?"

**Anticipated responses:**
- "Building was easier because you can feel the blocks!"
- "Drawing was harder because I had to imagine it!"
- "The words were confusing both times!"

---

### Game / Play (15 min) — "Mapmaker Challenges"

**Setup:** Kids work in teams of 3. Each team gets graph paper, pencils, and a set of blocks.

**Round 1 — Map the Classroom (5 min):**

**Teacher says:** "Your team is going to draw a floor plan of this room. Use the graph paper. Each square equals one big step. Start with the shape of the room, then add the big furniture."

**Teacher move:** Let teams roam the room. Some will pace out distances. Some will eyeball it. Both are valid strategies.

**Key question:** "How do you know how big to draw the teacher's desk compared to the door?"

**Round 2 — Block Challenge (5 min):**

Each team builds a structure with exactly 8 blocks. Then they draw THREE views: front, side, and top.

**Teacher says:** "Draw your structure from the front, from the side, and looking straight down from above. Label each drawing."

**Teacher move:** The top view (bird's eye) will be hardest. Suggest: "What if you put your eyes right above the blocks and looked straight down? What shapes would you see?"

**Round 3 — Mystery Build (5 min):**

Display a front view AND a top view of a structure on the board (drawn by you in advance).

**Teacher says:** "Here's the front view and the top view of a secret structure. Using your blocks, try to build something that matches BOTH views."

**Key insight:** There may be MORE THAN ONE structure that matches both views. This is a real-world problem in engineering — a profound realization that different 3D objects can produce the same 2D shadows.

**If kids discover this:** "Wait — ours is different from theirs but both match?!" This is gold. Celebrate it.

---

### Cool-Down & Journal (5 min)

**Teacher says:** "In your journal, draw a floor plan of your bedroom at home. Try to remember where your bed, desk, door, and window are. It doesn't have to be perfect — just try to show a bird's eye view."

**Alternative prompt:** "Draw a block structure and then draw its front view and top view."

**Sharing:** 2-3 volunteers show their bedroom floor plans. Ask: "Where is the door? Where do you walk from the door to your bed?" Tracing paths on floor plans is a natural extension.

---

### Chromebook Bonus (5 min) — "Floor Plan Builder"

**Teacher says:** "This week's game is Floor Plan Builder. You see a 3D room on the screen and you have to drag furniture to match a floor plan. Or you start with a floor plan and build the 3D room. It's like what we did today but on the computer."

**Game description:** Browser-based spatial reasoning game. Two modes: (1) Match mode — a floor plan is shown and kids drag 3D furniture into a room to match the layout. A bird's eye camera toggle lets them check their work. (2) Build mode — kids are shown a 3D room and must draw the floor plan on a grid. They can rotate the 3D view to check from different angles. Levels progress from rooms with 3 objects to rooms with 8+ objects, and eventually include two-story buildings where kids must draw floor plans for each level.

**Teacher says:** "Challenge for the week: Can you make a floor plan of your kitchen and bring it in next week? I'd love to see what you draw at home."

## Differentiation Notes

### If too easy:
- Challenge kids to draw views from all 4 sides (front, back, left, right) plus top
- Introduce "hidden blocks" — structures where some blocks are hidden behind others in certain views. "How many blocks COULD be in this structure? What's the minimum? Maximum?"
- Map challenge: draw the floor plan of the entire school (or at least their hallway)
- Coordinate grids: "The bookshelf is at position (3,5)" — bridge to coordinate systems

### If too hard:
- Perspective-taking: Use a single simple object (one block tower, 3 blocks tall) instead of a complex arrangement
- Build/describe: Start with only 3 blocks and very simple L-shapes
- Floor plans: Give a pre-drawn floor plan with blanks to fill in rather than starting from scratch
- Pair spatial thinkers with verbal thinkers — both skills are needed and the pairing helps both
- Provide a word bank: "on top of, next to, in front of, behind, to the left of, to the right of"
- For the map activity, let kids place actual small objects on graph paper rather than drawing

## Zvonkin Principles in This Lesson
1. **Questions > Answers:** "What does the other person see?" is genuinely open — there is no single right answer until you check.
2. **No explaining away:** The ambiguity of 2D views (multiple 3D structures match the same views) is left as a discovery, not resolved into a rule.
3. **Discovery is the goal:** The insight that perspective changes what you see is experienced physically, not lectured about.
4. **Manipulatives matter:** Blocks, toy figures, physical floor plans — spatial reasoning REQUIRES physical objects.
5. **Fun is non-negotiable:** Building with blocks is play. Describing structures is a communication game. Drawing maps of your bedroom is personal.
6. **Vary the packaging:** Same skill (spatial reasoning) appears as perspective-taking, descriptive building, mapmaking, and diagramming.
7. **Journal everything:** The bedroom floor plan is both a math exercise and a personal keepsake.
8. **Let chaos happen:** The build-from-description activity will produce hilarious mismatches. That's the lesson.

# Lesson 10: Math Circle Celebration

## Theme
Math Circle Celebration — revisiting favorites, teaching each other, and celebrating discoveries

## Learning Goals
- Reflect on the full arc of Math Circle: what we explored, what surprised us, what changed
- Experience the power of teaching — kids lead mini-lessons for each other
- Create original puzzles, demonstrating deep understanding
- Celebrate mathematical courage: the willingness to be wrong, to try, to wonder
- Leave with pride, joy, and the sense that math is something they OWN

## Zvonkin References
- Reflections on the circle experience and "diplomas": p. 76
- The importance of the social and emotional experience of math: throughout

## Timed Script

### Opening Bell (5 min) — "The Math Circle Memory Wall"

**Setup:** Before kids arrive, post 5 large sheets of poster paper on the walls, each labeled with a lesson theme:
1. "Magic Squares & Number Patterns" (Lesson 6)
2. "Topology & Shapes" (Lesson 7)
3. "Codes & Ciphers" (Lesson 8)
4. "Spatial Reasoning & Maps" (Lesson 9)
5. "Earlier Adventures" (Lessons 1-5)

Place markers near each poster.

**Teacher says:** "Welcome to our last Math Circle! Before we do anything else, I want you to walk around the room and write or draw ONE memory on each poster. It can be something you learned, something funny that happened, something that surprised you, or something you're still wondering about. You have 4 minutes. Go!"

**Teacher move:** Play upbeat music if you have it. Let kids roam freely. The energy should feel like a party, not a test.

**After 4 minutes:** Quickly read 2-3 highlights from each poster aloud. React with genuine enthusiasm. "Someone wrote 'I still don't understand the Mobius strip' — honestly, neither do most adults. That's what makes it great."

**Key question:** "Looking at all these posters — what was your favorite Math Circle day?"

(Take a quick hand-raise poll. Note the winner for Main Activity 1.)

---

### Main Activity 1 (15 min) — "Kids' Choice Stations"

**Setup:** Based on the poll (and your pre-session guess), set up 3-4 stations around the room, each featuring a favorite activity from the past weeks. Staff each station with materials and a brief instruction card.

**Suggested stations (adjust based on poll):**

**Station A — Magic Square Builder:**
- Number tiles 1-9, blank 3x3 grids
- Challenge card: "Build a magic square. Then try to build one with 9 in a corner."
- For kids who solved it before: "Can you explain to someone who hasn't done it?"

**Station B — Mobius Band Lab:**
- Paper strips, tape, scissors, markers
- Challenge card: "Make a Mobius band. Draw a line down the middle. Cut along the line. What happens? Now try giving the strip TWO twists before taping."

**Station C — Code Station:**
- Cipher wheels, index cards, alphabet-number keys
- Challenge card: "Write a secret message for a friend. Use any code you want. Leave the answer on the back."

**Station D — Block Build Challenge:**
- Blocks, barrier boards, graph paper
- Challenge card: "Build a structure with 10 blocks. Draw it from the front, side, and top."

**Teacher says:** "You'll rotate through stations. Spend about 4 minutes at each one. Here's the twist: if you've done the activity before and you're good at it, YOUR job is to TEACH someone who hasn't done it or who struggled. The best way to prove you understand something is to teach it."

**Teacher move:**
- Set a timer and call rotations every 4-5 minutes.
- Watch for kids teaching each other — this is the real assessment. A kid who can explain WHY 5 goes in the center of the magic square understands more than one who just memorizes the solution.
- Take notes (or photos) of kids teaching. These moments are gold for the diplomas.
- If a station is empty, that's data too — maybe that topic didn't land as well.

**Key questions to ask while circulating:**
- "Can you explain to [name] how you figured that out?"
- "What would you tell a kid who's never seen a Mobius band before?"
- "What's the first thing to try when you're cracking a code?"

---

### Main Activity 2 (15 min) — "Create Your Own Puzzle"

**Setup:** Kids return to their seats. Distribute "Puzzle Creation" worksheets and plenty of scratch paper.

**Teacher says:** "For the last nine weeks, I've been giving you puzzles. Now it's YOUR turn. You're going to create an original math puzzle that we can add to our Math Circle museum. It can be any kind of puzzle — a magic square with missing numbers, a secret code, a block-building challenge, a tricky question, a map to decode — anything mathematical."

**Worksheet prompts:**
1. **My Puzzle:** (Draw or write the puzzle here)
2. **The Answer:** (Write the answer on the back or on a separate flap)
3. **How Hard Is It?** (Circle one: Easy / Medium / Hard / Super Hard)
4. **My Name:** (So we know who the puzzle-master is)

**Teacher move:**
- Circulate and encourage. Some kids will dive in immediately. Others will freeze — "I don't know what to make!"
- For stuck kids, offer prompts:
  - "What if you made a magic square with some numbers missing?"
  - "What if you wrote a coded message for someone?"
  - "What if you described a block structure and challenged someone to build it?"
  - "What if you drew a map and asked a riddle about it?"
- For ambitious kids: "Can you make a puzzle that has MORE THAN ONE correct answer? That's really tricky to design."

**Key question:** "How do you know your puzzle is solvable? Have you checked that the answer works?"

**Teacher move for the last 3 minutes:** "If you're done, trade puzzles with a neighbor and try to solve theirs. If you can solve it, it works! If you can't, maybe it needs a tweak — or maybe you just need more time."

**Collect all puzzles for the Math Circle Museum display.**

---

### Game / Play (15 min) — "The Grand Math Circle Tournament"

**Setup:** A rapid-fire series of mini-challenges drawn from all previous lessons. Teams of 3. Keep score on the board (but emphasize fun over winning).

**Round 1 — Speed Magic Square (3 min):**
- Each team gets tiles 1-9 and a grid.
- First team to complete a correct magic square earns 3 points.
- Second team earns 2 points. Third earns 1.

**Round 2 — Crack the Code (3 min):**
- Write an encoded message on the board: "25-15-21   1-18-5   13-1-20-8   19-20-1-18-19" (YOU ARE MATH STARS)
- First team to decode it earns 3 points.

**Round 3 — Quick Build (3 min):**
- Show a front-view diagram of a simple block structure.
- Teams race to build it. First accurate build earns 3 points.

**Round 4 — Topology True or False (3 min):**
- Read statements. Teams hold up TRUE or FALSE cards (or thumbs up/down).
  - "A Mobius band has two sides." (FALSE — 1 point for each correct team)
  - "For any 3D shape, Vertices minus Edges plus Faces equals 2." (TRUE)
  - "Any map can be colored with 3 colors." (FALSE — you need 4)
  - "A donut and a coffee mug are the same shape in topology." (TRUE)
  - "A circle and a square are the same shape in topology." (TRUE)

**Round 5 — The Final Question (3 min):**
- "A farmer has a wolf, a goat, and a cabbage. He needs to cross a river in a boat that can only carry one thing besides himself. If he leaves the wolf with the goat, the wolf eats the goat. If he leaves the goat with the cabbage, the goat eats the cabbage. How does he get everything across?"
- Teams discuss. First team with a correct strategy earns 5 points.
- (Solution: Take goat across. Go back. Take wolf across. Bring goat back. Take cabbage across. Go back. Take goat across.)

**Announce winners. Applaud ALL teams.** "Everyone here is a winner because you spent 10 weeks doing real mathematics. Mathematicians around the world would be proud of you."

---

### Cool-Down & Journal (5 min) — "Final Reflections & Diplomas"

**Part A — Journal Entry (2 min):**

**Teacher says:** "One last journal entry. Finish this sentence: 'The most important thing I learned at Math Circle is...'"

**Alternative prompts:**
- "Math is..."
- "I used to think math was _______. Now I think math is _______."
- "My advice to a kid joining Math Circle for the first time is..."

**Teacher move:** Let kids write in silence for 2 minutes. This is their capstone reflection.

**Part B — Diploma Ceremony (3 min):**

**Teacher says:** "When mathematicians finish an important piece of work, they get recognized. You've all done 10 weeks of real mathematical thinking. I have something for each of you."

**Hand out Math Circle Diplomas one by one.** For each kid, say one specific, genuine thing:
- "Maya — you were the first person to figure out why 5 goes in the center of the magic square."
- "Jordan — your Mobius band experiment was incredible. You cut it three times and discovered something nobody else did."
- "Alex — you asked the best questions. 'What if we did this in binary?' changed the whole conversation."
- "Sam — you taught three other kids how to crack the Caesar cipher. That takes a real understanding."

**Diploma Template:**

```
===================================
    MATH CIRCLE DIPLOMA
===================================

This certifies that

    [CHILD'S NAME]

has completed 10 sessions of
mathematical exploration, discovery,
and wonder.

Special recognition for:
[PERSONALIZED NOTE]

Date: ___________
Signed: ___________
===================================
```

**Teacher move:** Make this feel ceremonial. Even silly kids will remember this moment. Take a group photo if possible.

## Differentiation Notes

### If too easy:
- Puzzle creation: Push ambitious kids to create multi-step puzzles or puzzles that combine ideas from different lessons (e.g., a coded message that reveals a magic square clue)
- Tournament: Add bonus questions worth extra points for kids who want more challenge
- Encourage these kids to think about starting a "lunch math club" or similar

### If too hard:
- Stations: Ensure each station has a "beginner" card with extra scaffolding
- Puzzle creation: Offer templates — a partially filled magic square they just need to finish, or a pre-drawn code key they fill in
- Tournament: Make some rounds collaborative (whole class vs. the teacher) rather than competitive
- Pair kids who struggled with patient peers during station work
- The diploma is CRUCIAL for these kids — make the personalized note about their effort, curiosity, or improvement, not just their "math skills"

### What to Save:
- Photograph the Memory Wall posters
- Collect all kid-created puzzles for a "Math Circle Puzzle Book" (you could compile and share with families)
- Save journal entries or photograph them if kids want to keep their journals
- Note which activities were most popular (for planning future circles)

### Communication to Families:
Consider sending home a note with each kid that includes:
- Their diploma
- A summary of what we covered in 10 weeks
- Links to all Chromebook games
- Suggestions for math play at home:
  - "Ask your child to show you a Mobius band"
  - "Try making a magic square together"
  - "Write secret messages in code"
  - "Draw a floor plan of your house"
- Book recommendation: Zvonkin's "Math from Three to Seven" for parents who want to continue

### Reflection for Next Time:
- Which lessons generated the most energy and engagement?
- Which activities fell flat or needed more scaffolding?
- Were there kids who bloomed late — who struggled early but found their stride?
- What would you change about the sequence?
- Did 60 minutes feel too long, too short, or just right for each segment?

## Zvonkin Principles in This Lesson
1. **Questions > Answers:** The puzzle creation flips the script — kids must think about what makes a good QUESTION, not just find answers.
2. **No explaining away:** Reviewing past activities, kids encounter things they still don't fully understand (the Mobius band, V-E+F=2). That's wonderful. Understanding deepens over years, not minutes.
3. **Discovery is the goal:** The discovery today is meta: kids discover that THEY are mathematicians.
4. **Manipulatives matter:** Every station has physical materials. Even in celebration, hands are busy.
5. **Fun is non-negotiable:** This is a party. Snacks, games, laughter, and diplomas.
6. **Vary the packaging:** The tournament repackages 10 weeks of content into a rapid-fire game.
7. **Journal everything:** The final journal entry is the most important one.
8. **Let chaos happen:** This session will be loud, energetic, and a little emotional. Exactly right.
